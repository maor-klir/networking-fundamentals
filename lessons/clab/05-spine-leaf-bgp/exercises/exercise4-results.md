### This confirms the full break chain end-to-end

1. leaf1 installs 10.20.4.0/25 --> blackhole
2. leaf1 advertises it to both spines
3. Spines propagate it to leaf2 and leaf3
4. leaf2 and leaf3 prefer /25 over /24 for 10.20.4.2 (longest-prefix-match)
5. Traffic is forwarded to leaf1, who drops it

## Explanation of longest-prefix-match hijacking: why a /25 wins over a /24 even though the /24 is the legitimate route

The longest-prefix-match is the most specific route, i.e. the route with the most bits matched. It always wins, regardless of where it came from.  
A `/25` matches 25 bits of the destination address. A `/24` matches only 24. For any destination in `10.20.4.0–10.20.4.127`, both routes match, but the `/25` will win every single time.  

This describes a potential attack. By advertising a more-specific prefix that overlaps the legitimate route, an attacker doesn't need to withdraw or outcompete the real route, they just need to be more specific.  
Every router that receives both prefixes silently prefers the `/25` for that address range, forwarding traffic to the attacker's blackhole instead of the real destination.  
The legitimate `/24` route stays in the routing table, it's just never used.
